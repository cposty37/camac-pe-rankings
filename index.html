Methodology & Data Provenance

How to Use This Dashboard

This tool narrows the global opportunity set to markets worth deeper investigation. The workflow: (1) use CVS and Access tier to identify statistically cheap, internationally accessible markets; (2) layer in GDP Growth and PEG-Lite to assess whether cheapness comes with genuine economic momentum; (3) check the Source column and data confidence tier before citing any figure externally; (4) apply standard due diligence — geopolitical risk, currency regime, liquidity, and sector composition are not captured here. Column headers are hoverable for per-metric context.

Critical caveat: Low P/E ≠ investable. Russia and Lebanon rank near the top by CVS but are effectively uninvestable (sanctions, active conflict). CVS is a statistical filter, not a risk-adjusted recommendation. Always layer with Access tier and source quality before acting on any signal.

Composite Value Score (CVS)

Definition: CVS = z-score of Earnings Yield (1 ÷ P/E) across all 94 scored markets at the time of the build. It answers: "How cheap is this market relative to the global cross-section?"

Interpretation guide: CVS +2.0 = earnings yield 2 standard deviations above the global mean — extreme statistical cheapness. CVS +1.0 = 1σ above mean (Deep Value threshold). CVS 0 = globally average. CVS −1.0 = 1σ below mean (Expensive). CVS −2.0 = very expensive relative to global peers.

Why z-score earnings yield? Raw P/E is hard to compare across markets with different sector compositions (resource-heavy markets structurally trade at lower P/Es than tech-heavy ones) and accounting standards. Z-scoring the earnings yield normalizes for the cross-sectional distribution at a point in time, making relative cheapness directly comparable regardless of absolute P/E levels.

What CVS does not capture: Political and sanctions risk · Currency convertibility and devaluation risk · Market liquidity and repatriation friction · Accounting standard differences (IFRS vs. local GAAP) · Data quality variance across sources · Sector composition differences. A market can have CVS +3.0 and still be functionally uninvestable.

Earnings Yield (EY%)

EY% = (1 ÷ P/E) × 100. The inverse of P/E — the equity market's analogue to a bond yield. At 8x P/E, the earnings yield is 12.5%; at 25x P/E, 4.0%. Useful for equity risk premium analysis: compare EY against local 10-year government bond yields to assess whether equities compensate for additional risk in that market. EY is the raw input to CVS before z-scoring.

PEG-Lite

Definition: PEG-Lite = P/E ÷ max(GDP Growth %, 1.0). Adapts the classic PEG ratio to country-level analysis. A market at 10x P/E growing at 7% GDP has PEG-Lite = 1.4x — cheap relative to growth. Values < 2.0x highlighted green; ≥ 2.0x amber. Shown in the Best Investable table only.

Why GDP growth, not EPS growth? Country-level forward earnings growth data is unavailable for most markets. GDP growth is the best available proxy for aggregate corporate earnings expansion potential over a cycle, particularly in markets where financials and consumer staples dominate the index.

Limitation: GDP growth from World Bank data, typically 1–2 years lagged. A market undergoing rapid recent growth may show a higher PEG-Lite than current conditions warrant. Treat as directional, not precise.

Economic Opportunity Score (0–100)

Scores ~58 countries without publicly available P/E data using 8 World Bank indicators. The question it answers: Which pre-market or illiquid economies have the structural conditions most likely to produce investable capital markets — and where is growth happening before exchanges open?

FactorMax PtsWhy This Factor, Why This Weight
GDP Growth25Highest weight. Raw growth is the primary signal for emerging capital market development. High-growth economies attract FDI, expand the corporate sector, and develop market infrastructure. Capped at 15% to reduce distortion from one-off commodity or post-war rebounds.
FDI / GDP20FDI inflows as % of GDP. When sophisticated foreign investors commit capital to a country, it validates the risk/return profile in a way that GDP alone cannot. FDI inflows are the strongest leading indicator of future exchange development — capital markets tend to formalize after foreign capital begins flowing in at scale.
Inflation Stability15Chronic high inflation destroys real returns, undermines the unit of account, and makes equity market formation nearly impossible (investors cannot price assets in a depreciating currency). Functions as a partial qualifier: ≤3% = 15 pts, stepwise to 0 pts above 20% CPI.
Population Scale12Market size proxy. Large domestic consumer bases generate the corporate ecosystem — retail, banking, telecom, consumer goods — that eventually lists on exchanges. Graduated from 1 pt (<1M) to 12 pts (>100M).
Trade Openness10(Exports + Imports) / GDP. Open economies develop financial services faster, face stronger corporate governance pressure from international counterparties, and historically develop deeper capital markets sooner than closed peers.
Population Growth8Demographic dividend proxy. Sweet spot: 0.5%–2.5% annual growth signals working-age population expansion, which drives consumer demand and labor supply. Near-zero or negative = demographic decline; >2.5% = development challenged by dependency ratios.
Internet Penetration5Digital infrastructure is a precondition for modern equity markets: retail brokerage, mobile banking, electronic trading, and price discovery all require internet access. Countries above 80% penetration score full points.
Savings Rate5Gross national savings as % of GNI. High domestic savings = a domestic pool of investable capital. Countries that develop equity markets need local institutional investors (pension funds, insurers) as a buyer base — which requires a savings culture. Countries above 30% score full points.

Requires ≥ 3 factors to produce a score. FDI/GDP requires both FDI and GDP total data. World Bank data typically lags 1–2 years. Note: this score intentionally excludes rule-of-law, corruption, and contract enforcement — important factors that lack consistent cross-country quantification in the World Bank dataset.

Access Tiers

  • Open — MSCI Developed or Emerging Market. Full institutional access; deep ETF and ADR ecosystem; high liquidity. No friction for long-only international investors.
  • Frontier — MSCI Frontier Market. Accessible to international investors but lower liquidity, higher transaction costs, and limited ETF options. Position sizing must account for exit friction.
  • Accessible — Non-MSCI market with a listed country ETF or major fund vehicle available to international investors. Investable with a vehicle, though the vehicle may have tracking error or thin liquidity itself.
  • Restricted — Non-MSCI with no accessible ETF or listed fund vehicle. Exchange exists and operates; direct international access requires local broker and custodian relationships. Investable in principle, operationally difficult.
  • Controlled — Capital controls, banking restrictions, or active financial crisis restricting international capital flow. Current examples: Lebanon (banking collapse), Ukraine (wartime controls), Argentina (multi-year capital controls), Myanmar (military sanctions), Zimbabwe (currency/access restrictions). CVS shown for monitoring purposes.
  • Inaccessible — De facto closed to international investors: active OFAC/UN sanctions or conflict-closed markets. Current examples: Russia, Belarus, Iran, Syria, Libya, Sudan, Venezuela, North Korea. CVS shown for analytical context only — not for investment action.

The Best Investable Opportunities section above excludes Controlled and Inaccessible markets, showing only Open + Frontier + Accessible markets.

Data Sources & Confidence Tiers

Zero hallucinated P/E data. Every figure traces to a cited source in the Source column. Confidence tiers, highest to lowest:

  • MSCI TTM (Float-adj) — Highest confidence. Trailing 12-month P/E from official MSCI country index factsheets. Float-adjusted, reconstituted quarterly. Covers all MSCI Developed, Emerging, and Frontier markets.
  • WFE Statistics — High confidence. World Federation of Exchanges quarterly market statistics reports, sourced directly from member exchanges.
  • AFC / Fund Portfolio (AFC, East Capital, Tundra, Rabee) — Medium confidence. Weighted harmonic average trailing P/E from Asia Frontier Capital, East Capital, Tundra Fonder, and Rabee Securities fund factsheets. Covers frontier markets not in MSCI (Vietnam, Uzbekistan, Iraq, and others). Fund P/E reflects portfolio holdings, not full-market coverage.
  • Cedar Capital / Specialist Fund Report — Medium confidence. Frontier and African market specialist fund quarterly reports. Currently used for Malawi (42.6x, Q3-2025). Authoritative for covered markets; review dates may lag current conditions by 1–2 quarters.
  • Local Index / Exchange Estimate — Lower confidence. P/E derived from local exchange publications, Bloomberg terminals, or analyst estimates. Use as directional signal; verify before citing externally.
  • SimplyWall.st / Retail Aggregator (Estimated) — Lowest confidence. Aggregated from stock-level data by a retail analytics platform; not a primary exchange source. Currently used for Namibia (6.6x, NSX) and Malta (10.5x, MSE). Verify against NSX annual statistics / MSE market report before citing in investment memos or client materials.
  • No Data — Exchange exists but no reliable composite P/E published by the exchange, MSCI, or any fund covering the market. pe_src field explains the specific reason. Shown for universe completeness.

ETF Data & World Bank Data Notes

ETF data (Div Yield, 1yr ETF return): fetched live via yfinance at build time. Reflects the primary country ETF's trailing dividend yield and 1-year total return in USD. Caveats: (1) ETF return ≠ local index return due to currency moves, management fees, and tracking differences; (2) NGE (Nigeria) and EGPT (Egypt) show null due to thin liquidity or near-delisting — treat those rows as Restricted for practical purposes; (3) ARGT (Argentina) data patched due to API rate limits.

World Bank data: fetched from the World Bank open API, covering 58 countries across 15 indicators. Data is typically 1–2 years lagged (most recent full-year available). Population data may show gaps in this build due to API availability at fetch time. Build date: March 13, 2026 · 18:55 ET.
